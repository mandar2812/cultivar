in-season yield forecasts Map How it works

How the forecasts work

These are in-season estimates of grain yield per acre. The general idea is to take everything known about the current season so far — weather, soils, planting progress — and combine it with an understanding of how crops respond to their environment to project what final yield will look like.

What goes into the estimate

  • Current-season weather — temperature, rainfall, and sunlight from the start of the year up to today.
  • Projected weather — plausible conditions for the rest of the growing season, based on historical patterns and adjusted for what we have observed so far.
  • Soil characteristics — the water-holding capacity and rooting depth of the soil at each location.
  • Planting and harvest timing — when farmers in each area typically get into the field, drawn from long-running surveys.

How they are produced

All of the above information is fed into a framework that relates growing conditions to yield outcomes. The framework captures how crops respond to heat, moisture, and other stresses in different situations.

Rather than producing a single number, the method generates a distribution of possible outcomes across thousands of representative locations in the region. County-level and state-level figures are obtained by averaging over these individual estimates.

Weather scenarios

Because the remainder of the season is unknown, the projection is repeated under several different assumptions about future weather:

  • Normal — what a typical season would look like, given what has happened so far.
  • Dry — a scenario where a drier, hotter historical year plays out for the rest of the season.
  • Hot — a scenario with anomalously high temperatures through the remainder of the growing window.
  • Wet — a scenario with above-normal rainfall to see how much excess moisture might matter.

Comparing across scenarios gives a sense of how sensitive the outcome is to the weather we might get in the coming weeks.

This is a research tool. Forecasts are not guaranteed and should not be used for financial or operational decisions.
